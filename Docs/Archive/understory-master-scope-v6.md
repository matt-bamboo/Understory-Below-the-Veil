## 0. Lock register

Nothing in this document changes without explicit agreement.

### Fully locked from v5

- Core circulation loop: descend -> extract -> ascend -> refine -> build -> wait -> return.
- Four design pillars: scarcity makes placement sacred; the wait has a place to go; one tool has two verbs; the past is the building material.
- Scarcity from the dig. Nothing rare is purchasable.
- No coins, no abstract currency, ever.
- Dig-unlocks-land coupling: completing or restoring a stratum below opens habitable land above.
- Depth thresholds earn new dig sites inside one persistent world.
- Ghost maps discovered underground and rebuilt in place.
- Free blueprint drafting: ghost blueprints can be drafted anywhere, anytime, at zero cost. Commit later with real material.
- Collapse never destroys. It buries. Re-excavation recovers material and can reveal hidden finds.
- Rarity equals depth plus care.

- Crew timers: player-assigned jobs with multiple duration options per action.
- Patient monetization onboarding: no store surface in the first sessions.
- Authored first descent: first hard stop, first find, first shoring decision, first return, and first build happen in a designed sequence before real timer complexity starts.
- Return Ritual, Core Sample, careful traced-seam extraction, expressive underground light, Lapse Ceremony, and Archive shelf/hall remain core product pieces.

### Fully locked in v6

- Understory is the master. HEM is no longer the frame. HEM contributes feel, not structure.
- Present date: **AD 9026**.
- Tone lock: **The world is not post-apocalyptic misery. It is patient, strange, materially hopeful, and human. Damage is not erased. It is mended, reinforced, lit, inhabited, and made beautiful.**
- The current people live on a great high mountain above **the Veil**, a lowland cloud/haze/toxic-weather layer that hides the lower world and makes exterior descent dangerous.
- The world has recovered more than humanity has. The recovery engine is a combination of **the Long Cooling** and **the Drawdown Works**.
- The Long Cooling is the planet-scale background. The Drawdown Works are ancient atmospheric repair systems that still breathe beneath the earth.
- The Drawdown Works did not simply save the world. Their damaged, partial, or misaligned operation helped create or sustain the Veil.
- The Bore is a found ancient shaft at the summit. It gives the summit people controlled access to deep minerals, rare earths, water, old machines, and buried history.
- The surface journey is **the Carve**: the player begins at the summit and carves downward outside the mountain as the Veil clears.
- The underground journey is **the Bore into the Hollow**: crews descend through the found shaft to deep footholds, then excavate and build upward through ancient halls and mountain systems.
- Exterior progression: surface descent reveals future human decline in reverse, from AD 9026 toward the Low Before.
- Underground progression: below ground is ancient-first, mainly c. 8000 BC through AD 2000, with later Hollower and current-crew overlays.
- The recent ancestors, working name **the Hollowers**, did not build the core underground civilization. They reused, patched, occupied, misunderstood, and eventually sealed it.

- The original Bore may be far older than known Homo sapiens. The late reveal is the **Before-Us / Otherhuman** layer, currently penciled at about 5,000,000 BC. This remains late-game narrative gravity, not onboarding.
- The major underground identity is ancient original architecture plus later overlays. Recent use should appear as fingerprints, repairs, warnings, bunks, and sealed doors, not as the main architecture.
- The Veil clears in bands when the player restores Old Lungs and reconnects Breathlines. Surface land unlocks should feel like the mountain learning to breathe again.
- The Far-Glass and Far Peak are part of canon: another mountain civilization may be visible later through a telescope-like device, first as a distant light.
- The game should remain an iOS-first tapper-builder with epic implication, not a console-first cinematic adventure.

## 1. One-paragraph pitch

Understory is a touch-first vertical restoration builder set in AD 9026 on a great high mountain above the Veil. Humanity has survived in thin air for generations while the lower world disappeared beneath cloud, poison weather, myth, and fear. The planet has slowly recovered through the Long Cooling and the buried Drawdown Works, ancient atmospheric machines still breathing under the earth, but the summit people cannot see or safely reach the world below. When a sealed Bore opens at the mountain's crown, crews descend into the Hollow and discover ancient halls, scarce materials, Old Lungs, Breathlines, ghost maps, and the buried record of human rise and fall. Below, the player excavates upward through history. Above, the player carves downward into newly visible land. Every block placed above came from below. Every restored machine clears more of the Veil. Every terrace is one more piece of the world earned back.

## 2. Product identity

### The practical product

Understory is still a tapper-builder / idle-builder hybrid. It is not becoming a console RPG, combat adventure, or lore-first walking simulator.

The story is epic. The interaction stays intimate:

- Check the crew.
- Resolve a hard stop.
- Trace a seam.
- Return the haul.
- Refine material.
- Commit part of a draft.
- Watch a tiny piece of the mountain become livable.

### The emotional product

The player is not just building a town. They are helping humanity earn the world back.

The first emotional truth: **we need the mountain to live.**

The second emotional truth: **the mountain remembers people.**

The third emotional truth: **the mountain remembers us.**

The fourth emotional truth: **we were not the first us.**

The final emotional turn: **the past ends, and now the player builds the next layer.**

### Why iOS first still holds

The core verbs are born on glass:

- Drag a rounded block.
- Draft a ghost wall.
- Trace a seam slowly.
- Tap a haul crate.
- Inspect a Core Sample.
- Open the Far-Glass at night.

Pocket play handles return, jobs, haul, trace, and small commits. Table play handles building, drafting, decorating, and larger restoration sessions.

## 3. Design pillars

1. **Scarcity makes placement sacred.** Every block was pulled from below, refined above, and chosen by the player.
2. **The wait has a place to go.** Idle time is not dead time. It becomes drafting, planning, and wanting.
3. **One tool, two verbs.** The Stitcher or equivalent field tool unmakes downward and remakes upward with the same touch grammar.
4. **The past is the building material.** Narrative is carried by strata, artifacts, ghost maps, and rebuilt structures, not cutscenes.
5. **The mountain breathes.** Restoring Old Lungs and Breathlines clears the Veil, reveals land, and turns machine repair into world recovery.
6. **Human first, planetary second, cosmic third.** The player cares about the summit people before the game asks them to care about deep time.

## 4. World geometry: the Mirrored Nautilus

The world is one mountain surrounded by ocean, forested lowlands, and the obscuring Veil. The player experiences two spirals joined by the summit.

### The Carve: outside descent

The player begins at the summit and opens terraces downward. The Carve is the surface settlement path: a partial nautilus around the mountain, widening as the Veil clears.

What it means:

- New land is not bought or passively unlocked. It is revealed and made safe by underground restoration.
- Each terrace shows exposed strata and history.
- Surface descent reads as the reverse fall of humanity: from AD 9026 summit survival back toward the Low Before.
- The player is not just expanding a base. They are returning to the world.

### The Bore: vertical access

The Bore is a found ancient shaft at the mountain crown. It was sealed, mythologized, and partly forgotten. It drops into the Hollow.

What it means:

- The summit people could not safely descend outside through the Veil, but the Bore gives controlled access.
- The Bore is older than the recent ancestors.
- The Bore may be older than known humanity.
- The Bore is the reason the scarce summit suddenly gains minerals, water, masonry, rare earths, ancient machines, and buildable futures.

### The Hollow: inside ascent

The Hollow is the deep cavern where crews establish footholds and begin building upward through ancient halls and mountain systems.

What it means:

- Outside, the player descends.
- Inside, the player climbs.
- The two journeys should eventually approach each other near the Base / Confluence.

### Spatial landmarks

- The Summit Refuge: current home.
- The Shafthead: the mandatory hub around the Bore.
- The Core Sample: the truth column that fills with strata.
- The Archive: where Singulars and story fragments accumulate.
- The Far-Glass: telescope-like prop that later reveals Far Peak.
- The Carve: outside terraces downward.
- The Bore: vertical ancient throat.
- The Hollow: inner dark lake and reverse climb.
- The Base: long-term reveal of the old low world.

## 5. Canon and timeline

### The short version

In AD 9026, the summit people live above the Veil. The lower world has been hidden for generations. The Long Cooling and the Drawdown Works have helped the planet recover, but the mountain people do not understand that recovery because the Veil still hides it. When the Bore opens, they discover that the mountain is a memory trap. Ancient humans used it around 8000 BC. Later civilizations revisited it. Recent ancestors occupied and sealed it. The current player opens it again.

### Underground ascent: the rise of human capability

The underground should be ancient-first. The major halls, lungs, ghost maps, and awe structures come from the ancient layers. Later use is overlay.

#### Layer 0: Before-Us Threshold, c. 5,000,000 BC, late game

Emotional purpose: prove that human-like culture has happened before, without making the early game about aliens.

What the player sees:

- Doorways and rest spaces almost made for us, but not quite.
- Hearth-like rooms with no fire marks.
- Tools that feel like stone, bone, and interface at once.
- Domestic objects from a culture that was not modern human but still recognizably caring.
- Architecture that feels like a home before it feels like a machine.

Why it matters: this turns the deepest reveal into empathy, not monster lore.

Risk: do not reveal too early.

#### Layer 1: Breathwright Origin, c. 8000 BC to 6500 BC

Emotional purpose: this is Understory's ancient magic-tech signature.

What the player sees:

- Stone lungs.
- Breathlines.
- Carbon-black vaults.
- Pressure doors shaped like ritual gates.
- Star shafts that also calibrate air and light.
- Acoustic chambers where sound wakes vents.
- Water bowls that are also atmospheric sensors.
- Mineral-thread looms and hand-worn control stones.

Why it matters: their technology survived as ritual because ritual was the only interface durable enough to cross collapse.

Risk: do not explain the tech to death. Let the first Old Lung feel like a sacred machine.

#### Layer 2: Seed and Calendar Halls, c. 6500 BC to 4500 BC

Emotional purpose: make the ancient world feel lived-in and caring, not just monumental.

What the player sees:

- Seed vaults.
- Crop calendars.
- Water clocks.
- Soil chambers.
- Underground gardens.
- Flood prediction basins.
- Nurseries, storage niches, and food-preservation rooms.

Why it matters: this layer says people were feeding children down here.

Risk: avoid full farming sim creep early.

#### Layer 3: Monument Civic Age, c. 4500 BC to 2500 BC

Emotional purpose: give the underground grandeur without borrowing real Egypt.

What the player sees:

- Processional ramps.
- Star-aligned shafts.
- Memorial chambers.
- Public stone records.
- Ritual waterworks.
- Ancestor walls.
- Giant ghost maps of halls, cisterns, and chambers.

Why it matters: this is spectacle that still fits an iPad builder.

Risk: no ancient-aliens framing, no claim that real ancient cultures were secretly built by hidden tech.

#### Layer 4: Furnace and Alloy Age, c. 2500 BC to 800 BC

Emotional purpose: fire enters the mountain.

What the player sees:

- Copper-green stains.
- Bronze fittings.
- Early furnaces.
- Slag glass.
- Bellows chambers.
- Casting molds.
- Metal-lined ducts.
- Tool shrines.

Why it matters: this layer maps cleanly to material unlocks, forge upgrades, shoring, and lift capability.

Risk: avoid recipe spreadsheet complexity.

#### Layer 5: Road, Water, and Law Age, c. 800 BC to AD 500

Emotional purpose: show society, coordination, and public infrastructure.

What the player sees:

- Aqueduct-like channels.
- Public cisterns.
- Road galleries.
- Mile markers.
- Civic seals.
- Water-right tablets.
- Standardized blocks.
- Assembly chambers.

Why it matters: this layer makes ghost maps functional: restored roads improve movement, restored cisterns enable water systems.

Risk: no government sim.

#### Layer 6: Quiet Orders, AD 500 to AD 1400

Emotional purpose: preservation and memory.

What the player sees:

- Scriptoria.
- Dry libraries.
- Lamp niches.
- Map rooms.
- Lens fragments.
- Quiet sleeping cells.
- Songs encoded as work instructions.
- Prayer-machine hybrids.

Why it matters: this is where the Archive grows beyond storage into identity.

Risk: avoid text-heavy lore dumps.

#### Layer 7: Glass and Mechanism Age, AD 1400 to AD 1750

Emotional purpose: humanity starts measuring, seeing, and suspecting the mountain's larger role.

What the player sees:

- Lenses.
- Mirrors.
- Brass mechanisms.
- Clockwork vents.
- Star charts.
- Pressure gauges.
- Calibration wheels.
- Early Far-Glass roots.

Why it matters: this connects the telescope and Far Peak mystery to deeper history.

Risk: avoid puzzle-box bloat.

#### Layer 8: Steam and Smoke Age, AD 1750 to AD 1910

Emotional purpose: the mountain begins to feel used harder, louder, and less carefully.

What the player sees:

- Rails.
- Boilers.
- Soot ducts.
- Pump houses.
- Piston lifts.
- Iron pipe retrofits.
- Cracked ancient stone where newer systems were forced through.

Why it matters: this layer shows the difference between integrated ancient systems and extractive industrial use.

Risk: do not let steampunk aesthetics take over the whole identity.

#### Layer 9: Wire, Atomic, and Digital Edge, AD 1910 to AD 2000

Emotional purpose: recognizable shock.

What the player sees:

- Cables.
- Sensors.
- Broken monitors.
- Lab doors.
- Warning labels.
- Server ruins.
- Early climate models.
- Environmental instruments.
- Small human objects: badge, mug, photo, hard hat, field recorder.

Why it matters: the player suddenly realizes modern humanity is also just one layer.

Risk: use sparingly. If the underground fills with screens, the ancient mood dies.

### Overlays

#### Hollower overlay, AD 7600 to AD 8600

Recent ancestors leave:

- Bedrolls.
- Patched pipes.
- Warning paint.
- Family marks.
- Sealed doors.
- Cooking corners.
- Broken lift platforms.
- Proto-summit language.

They are not the builders. They are the failed return.

#### Current crew overlay, AD 9026 onward

The player and crew add:

- Lantern strings.
- Chalk marks.
- Fresh braces.
- Haul crates.
- New pipes.
- New ghost drafts.
- Current crew notes.

The player is writing the newest layer of the memory trap.

## 6. The Veil, Old Lungs, and Breathlines

### The Veil

The Veil is a lowland layer of cloud, toxic haze, industrial aerosol residue, machine exhaust, humidity, storm inversion, spores, and cultural terror. It is not carbon dioxide. Carbon dioxide is invisible. The Veil is the visible and dangerous symptom of broken atmosphere systems, geography, and memory.

### The Old Lungs

The Old Lungs are ancient Drawdown Works. They pull, bind, mineralize, vent, cool, and rebalance. The summit people understand them through myth: lungs, bellies, throats, breath-stones, sleeping organs.

### Breathlines

Breathlines are ducts, pipes, vents, tunnels, cistern channels, and pressure routes that connect Old Lungs to terraces and shafts.

### Gameplay function

- Restore a Breathline ghost map below.
- Wake or stabilize an Old Lung.
- The Veil thins from a surface band.
- A new terrace becomes visible and safe enough to carve.
- The Far-Glass gains a clearer sightline.

### Scope warning

Do not build a freeform Factorio pipe sim early. First Breathlines are authored ghost-map repairs with clear cause and effect.

## 7. The core loop

### Loop

1. **Descend:** workers have progressed while away. New strata, hazards, hard stops, or finds may be revealed.
2. **Extract:** player makes the decisions machines cannot: where to cut, what to shore, which seam to trace, whether to blast bulk or preserve rare material.
3. **Ascend:** the haul arrives at the shafthead through the Return Ritual.
4. **Refine:** raw material enters kiln, sawmill, forge, cutter, or later age-specific refiners.
5. **Build:** the player places real blocks or commits ghost drafts.
6. **Wait:** dig and refine clocks run offline with caps.
7. **Return:** the crew note, haul table, Core Sample, and drafts tell the player what matters next.

### Session shapes

- 30 seconds: check haul, read note, assign job, commit a few blocks.
- 2 to 5 minutes: resolve hard stop, trace seam, repair support, choose next route.
- 10 minutes: refine, commit a small draft, decorate, inspect Archive.
- 20+ minutes: iPad building session, ghost map rebuild, terrace design.

### First 10 minutes

1. Player sees Summit Refuge above the Veil.
2. Sealed Bore opens.
3. First descent is authored.
4. Player extracts first material and sees one ancient hint.
5. Return Ritual brings haul to surface.
6. Player refines and commits first drafted shelter, stair, or shafthead wall.
7. The Core Sample gains its first band.
8. The player sees the Veil as the reason more land matters.

## 8. Tapper and idle math rationale

This is design math, not final tuning. Telemetry will replace it.

### Where the math comes from

The math is driven by the intended session shapes and emotional output, not by a copied economy table.

Known product targets:

- A casual daily player should place about 60 to 120 meaningful blocks per day early.
- A 10-minute daily player should make a room, not a castle.
- A castle-scale build should take weeks so it matters.
- A first-day player should complete a visible shelter or shafthead repair without paying.
- A returning player should always have something to do even when material-poor because drafting is free.

### Working unit definitions

- **Placed Block Equivalent (PBE):** one committed build cell after refining.
- **Raw Material Unit (RMU):** one extractable unit before refining.
- **Refined Material Unit (FMU):** one committed building unit after refining.
- **Haul Value:** RMU plus rare-find chance plus story/hard-stop chance.
- **Want-list Pressure:** the gap between drafted FMU need and current FMU inventory.

### Job duration ladder, first tuning hypothesis

| Job type | Duration | Player use | Design note |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Touch scout | 2 to 3 min | first-session and quick check-in | reveals decision, low yield |
| Short cut | 10 to 15 min | lunch-break return | useful yield, frequent notes |
| Work shift | 1 to 2 hr | normal day loop | main progress unit |
| Long shift | 4 hr | half-day plan | slightly less efficient, convenient |
| Overnight shift | 8 hr | sleep/work gap | capped and reliable |
| Long absence cap | 8 to 12 productive hr | lapse protection | beyond cap, Lapse Ceremony creates story hook |

### Reward curve principle

Longer jobs should be convenient, not strictly better. Shorter jobs should feel lively, not mandatory. A simple target curve:

- Yield grows with duration.
- Efficiency gently decreases with duration.
- Rare/hard-stop chance increases with depth and authored content, not with payment.
- Careful extraction improves find integrity and rare quality, not bulk volume.

### Early economy target

| Milestone | Target output |
|---|---|
| First 10 minutes | first shelter/wall segment, Core Sample band, Archive shelf seed |
| Day 1 | 80 to 180 total PBE available, enough for a small shafthead improvement and drafts |
| Day 3 | first meaningful ghost draft want-list, first shortage the player authored |
| Day 7 | first terrace reveal or strong pre-reveal, first Old Lung/Breathline anticipation |
| Day 30 | one complete age page, one major ghost map rebuild, one signature room/tower/terrace |

### Why free drafting is non-negotiable

Without drafting, scarcity says: come back later.

With drafting, scarcity says: now you know what to dig for.

### Metrics to instrument

- Time to first block.
- Time to first draft.
- Time to first material shortage.
- Time to first Return Ritual skip.
- Number of draft blocks placed per material-poor session.
- Percent of players who return after a hard stop.
- Percent of players who return after Lapse Ceremony.
- D1, D7, D30 retention.
- Subscriber conversion after first attachment moment.
- Store open after non-pinch-point presentation.
- Refund and churn after subscription ask.

Every material should have a mood, a provenance, and a use. If it is only a skin, cut it or delay it.

## 10. Surface systems

### Summit Refuge

The starting home is a cramped, high-altitude refuge around the sealed Bore.

It should show:

- Thin-air culture.
- Water catchers.
- Patched ceramic shelters.
- Wind-worn surfaces.
- Old antenna bones or salvaged frames.
- Far-Glass pointed at the Veil.
- A sealed shafthead that feels feared and important.

### Land unlock

Land unlock is not an arbitrary expansion. It is the visible retreat of the Veil after an underground restoration.

First reveal sequence:

1. Restore a broken Breathline below.
2. Old Lung pulses.
3. Air or light travels upward/outward.
4. Surface Veil thins.
5. A lower ledge or terrace becomes visible.
6. Player can carve, draft, and build there.

### Free drafting

Drafting remains the pressure valve.

Player can draft:

- Summit shelter additions.
- Terrace walls.
- Lantern paths.
- Archive hall.
- Breathline endpoints.
- Ghost-map completions.

Drafts become want-lists for the next dig.

## 11. Underground systems

### Hard stops

Hard stops are warm friction, not punishment.

Examples:

- Sealed ancient door.
- Unstable ceiling.
- Traceable rare seam.
- Hollower warning mark.
- Broken Breathline.
- Water seep.
- Void edge.
- Ghost map footprint.
- Old Lung chamber.

### Careful extraction

One traced gesture:

- Trace the seam slowly.
- Too fast: visual flare, haptic warning on iPhone.
- Complete trace: intact find.
- Blast: bulk material, less integrity.

### Support and collapse

Collapse never destroys. It buries. Re-excavation recovers. Sometimes collapse reveals hidden chambers or finds.

### Water

Water is Phase 1, not Phase 0 lead.

First water goal:

- One seep hard stop.
- One pump/Breathline/cistern ghost map.
- One drained chamber reward.

### Light

Light remains expressive first. Darkness is not hostile.

Lantern strings make the mountain human again.

## 12. Archive, Core Sample, and memory trap

### Core Sample

The Core Sample is the signature object at the shafthead. It fills with cross-sections of every stratum and contradiction the player discovers.

Role:

- Progress object.
- Screenshot object.
- Age index.
- Truth column that eventually challenges summit myth.

### Archive

The Archive is the home for Singulars, fragments, myths, and evidence.

It should preserve conflicting versions:

- Summit myth.
- Hollower warning.
- Ancient inscription.
- Physical material evidence.
- Player reconstruction.

The Archive should not feel like an encyclopedia. It should feel like a room full of objects that slowly disagree with each other.

### Memory trap principle

The mountain is not just a place. It is a recurring refuge. Civilizations keep rediscovering it when the world becomes desperate.

Question beneath the game:

Are we restoring the mountain, or are we being invited to repeat it?

## 13. Monetization that can make money without alienating players

### The money covenant

Understory can make money, but not by betraying the premise.

Locked:

- No coins.
- No abstract premium currency.
- No loot boxes.
- No gacha.
- No purchasable rare materials.
- No purchasable land.
- No paid rescue at collapse, flood, hard stop, or scarce-material frustration.
- No store in the first sessions.
- No paid advantage that makes earned blocks feel fake.

### Revenue stack

1. **Subscription as primary model.** Working name: Expedition Pass or Hearth Pass. Buys breadth, convenience, continuity, cosmetics, archive niceties, extra queues, no ads, and ongoing chapter value.
2. **Direct named crew purchases.** One-time characters, bought directly, owned forever. No randomization. Each adds voice, note flavor, scheduling breadth, and light specialty. They should not create exclusive material access.
3. **Seasonal expedition shafts / chapter packs.** Paid authored content: new age band, new materials, new ghost maps, new Archive page, new visual finish family. Subscribers may get included access or a discount. Exact packaging open.
4. **Cosmetic finish packs.** Patinas, glazes, lantern styles, Archive pedestals, material dressings. Same function, different expression.
5. **Refine-queue-only skips.** Low-corrosion but risky. Never apply to dig, rare finds, hard stops, land, or story gates. Keep optional, explicit, and real-money priced if used.
6. **Rewarded ads, opt-in only, non-subscribers only.** No forced ads. No ad at hard stops. Subscription removes them.
7. **Founder / Patron purchase.** One-time support bundle: ad removal forever plus cosmetic family and visible patron object.
8. **Extra world slots.** Useful for builders, low corrosion.

### Working price hypotheses

These are not locks.

| Item | Candidate price | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Monthly pass | $5.99 to $7.99 | needs ongoing content and clear value |
| Annual pass | $39.99 to $59.99 | best if live ops cadence is real |
| Named crew | $3.99 to $9.99 | direct purchase, no duplicates |
| Cosmetic finish pack | $1.99 to $4.99 | small, expressive, no function |
| Seasonal expedition | $7.99 to $14.99 | depending on content scale |
| Founder bundle | $19.99 to $39.99 | early supporter, ad removal, cosmetics |

### Net revenue scaffold

Apple's subscription model pays 70 percent of subscription price during a subscriber's first paid year and 85 percent after one year, before taxes and adjustments. This matters for the five-figure-month target.

Example scaffold using $6.99 monthly subscription:

- Gross monthly price: $6.99.
- First-year net before tax: about $4.89.
- Subscribers needed for $10,000 net/month from subscription alone: about 2,045.
- If 3 percent of MAU subscribe, that implies about 68,200 MAU.
- If 5 percent of MAU subscribe, that implies about 40,900 MAU.

More realistic month-18 mix:

| Source | Example assumption | Approx. net/month |
|---|---:|---:|
| Subscription | 50k MAU, 3.5 percent subscribe, $6.99 gross | ~$8.6k before tax |
| Named crew / cosmetics | 1.5 percent buyer rate, modest ARPPU | ~$2k to $5k |
| Seasonal chapter spikes | launch windows | variable |
| Rewarded ads | non-sub casual users only | small support, not core |

Target: durable five-figure months by month 18 requires either meaningful MAU, strong subscription conversion, or seasonal content that players trust enough to buy. The product should not chase whales. It should chase attachment.

### Anti-alienation rules

- Show real money prices directly.
- Keep the free path whole.
- Subscriber gets more ways to enjoy the game, not a different moral economy.
- Paid crew should add warmth and scheduling breadth, not become mandatory optimization pieces.
- No artificial frustration inserted to sell relief.
- Every purchase surface should appear in calm spaces: Archive, cosmetic shelf, crew lodge, season notice, settings. Never at pain points.

## 14. IP and attraction strategy

### Recommendation

Do not license a major external IP for the first product. Understory's core premise is now strong enough to stand as original IP, and a license would likely add cost, approval friction, brand mismatch, and creative compromise before the loop is proven.

### Better approach

Use public-domain and open cultural resonance as inspiration or optional event theming, after legal review.

Potential public-domain inspiration directions:

| Source direction | Why it fits | Use carefully |
|---|---|---|
| Journey to the Center of the Earth | direct descent myth, geological wonder | inspiration or event tone, not title dependency |
| At the Earth's Core / hollow-earth pulp | underground adventure recognition | avoid pulp combat/slavery baggage |
| The Time Machine | far-future humanity and layered civilization | use as thematic echo, not crossover unless clear |
| The Lost World | hidden plateau/world discovery | more surface expedition than core loop |
| The Coming Race | subterranean advanced society | interesting but culturally dated, requires care |
| Public Domain Day 2026 works | marketing event potential | only use works whose public-domain status and trademarks are cleared |

### Licensing test

Only consider licensed IP if it passes all of these:

- It can bring low-cost user acquisition.
- It does not distort the Veil, Bore, Ancient Human, or tapper-builder identity.
- It does not require story approvals that slow live operations.
- It does not force combat, characters, or fan-service that weakens the game.
- It can be used as a seasonal expedition without making the original world feel secondary.

### Practical call

Original IP first. Public-domain-inspired events later. Paid license only after retention is proven.

## 18. Risks

1. Narrative over-density. Mitigation: first playable only teaches need, Veil, Bore, material, build.
2. Idle math fragility. Mitigation: prototype economy early and instrument heavily.
3. Authored age content treadmill. Mitigation: reuse material families, draft pressure, Archive collection, seasonal cadence.
4. Monetization trust break. Mitigation: no coins, no gacha, no pain-point offers, direct real-money clarity.
5. Scope drift into console epic. Mitigation: iOS-first touch loops, short sessions, mockups before engine pivots.
6. Public-domain/IP confusion. Mitigation: original IP first, legal review before any public-domain event.
7. Spiral geometry confusion. Mitigation: strong landmarks, Core Sample, readable map, simple early path.
8. The Veil becoming a survival sim. Mitigation: visual/narrative blocker first, no gas meters early.
9. Ancient layer becoming conspiracy-coded. Mitigation: fictional resonance, no real-culture replacement claims.
10. Return Ritual fatigue. Mitigation: ten-second scene, skippable once familiar.
